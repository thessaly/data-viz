# Country Profile: Greece

*UNESCO 2021 Recommendation on Open Science — Survey Responses*

---

## Key Indicators

**R&D expenditure (% GDP):** As of December 2024, the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) reports that Greece's gross domestic expenditure on research and development (R&D) remains at 1.3% of its Gross Domestic Product (GDP). See: https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2024/12/oecd-economic-surveys-greece-2024_26a6b3e5/a35a56b6-en.pdf

**Internet access rate:** As of early 2024, approximately 86.9% of Greek households have internet access. Efforts are ongoing to enhance digital infrastructure, as outlined in the National Broadband Plan 2021-2027 (https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/broadband-greece), which aims to promote the use of fixed very high-capacity and 5G networks as catalysts for the country's digital transformation.

## B1 – Promotion of the UNESCO 2021 Recommendation on Open Science

### B1.1 — If yes, please indicate which ministries and or national authorities/entities have been involved in the promotion of the 2021 Recommendation

On February 15, 2022, an online event titled “UNESCO: Open Science – Artificial Intelligence. New Horizons, Visions, and Boundaries” was held to present the UNESCO Recommendations on Open Science to the Greek scientific community (https://elpida.org/%CE%B4%CE%B9%CE%B1%CE%B4%CE%B9%CE%BA%CF%84%CF%85%CE%B1%CE%BA%CE%B7-%CE%B7%CE%BC%CE%B5%CF%81%CE%B9%CE%B4%CE%B1-unesco-%CE%B1%CE%BD%CE%BF%CE%B9%CF%87%CF%84%CE%AE-%CE%B5%CF%80%CE%B9%CF%83%CF%84/).  Additionally, the Recommendations were communicated to events where other Ministries were present such as at the 1st Greek EOSC Tripartite Event in October 2022 (https://eosc.eu/news/greek-open-science-community-gathered-tripartite-event), and the 3rd Open Science Symposium and EOSC Tripartite Event in Greece in December 2024 (https://eosc.eu/news/2024/12/national-tripartite-event-in-greece-highlights-community-as-the-driving-force-for-open-science/). The latter was held under the joint auspices of the Ministries of Development, of Digital Governance, and of Education, Religious Affairs and Sports.

### B1.3 — If yes, please provide details on the activities organized or foreseen and links as relevant

Established in February 2022 by 13 leading Greek research, technology, and innovation organisations, the Hellenic Open Science Initiative (HOSI) aims to implement Open Science policies in Greece and support national representation in the European Open Science Cloud (EOSC). Among other things, HOSI’s Training Working group has been active in preparing and promoting training material (see: https://zenodo.org/communities/os-greece/records?q=&l=list&p=1&s=10&sort=newest & https://palc30.lib.uoa.gr/%cf%80%cf%81%ce%bf%ce%b3%cf%81%ce%b1%ce%bc%ce%bc%ce%b1-%cf%83%cf%85%ce%bd%ce%b5%ce%b4%cf%81%ce%b9%ce%bf%cf%85-2/) as well as co-organised the training events on the second day of the 2024 Open Science Symposium. OpenAIRE plays a key role in promoting Open Science in Greece through its National Open Access Desk (NOAD), hosted by Athena Research Center, providing training, policy support, and infrastructure development. The Greek NOAD has been promoting Open Science and spreading awareness on new developments and services for implementation since 2009 and collaboratively organising webinars with the Cypriot NOAD. Some statistics can be found here: https://zenodo.org/records/14944907  Additionally, iIndividual HOSI members are active in European projects that support the adoption and implementation of Open Science which facilitates knowledge transfer to their institutions and the country at large. For more statistics and progress on how Greece and other countries perform in EOSC can be found at the EOSC Observatory: https://eoscobservatory.eosc-portal.eu/home.

### B1.4 — If yes, please indicate the actions that are taken to incorporate the specific values and principles. For each case, please provide details and links as relevant

A new version of the National Open Science Plan (https://zenodo.org/records/14808290), currently under review by Greek authorities (Ministry of Digital Governance, MERAS, and GSRI), lays the groundwork for a comprehensive Open Science Strategy. The plan covers key areas such as open access, research data, software development, and e-infrastructures, detailing 62 actions that include infrastructure development, education policies, funding mechanisms, and legislative measures. The Hellenic Open Science Initiative (HOSI) plays a central role in shaping and implementing this strategy, bringing together researchers, library organisations, and technology providers to foster an open and collaborative research environment. Concrete steps are being taken to integrate Open Science principles into publicly funded research. Efforts are underway to include Open Science requirements in funding calls by the GSRI and the Hellenic Foundation for Research & Innovation (HFRI), with a particular emphasis on ensuring Open Access to scientific publications.  These actions align with the values and guiding principles of Open Science, including quality and integrity, equity and fairness, transparency, responsibility, collaboration, and sustainability, ensuring that Open Science becomes a core component of Greece’s research ecosystem by 2025.

## B2 – Open Science Policies and Governance

### B2.1 — Share a machine-readable PDF file of the policy

Title of the Policy: Law 4310/2014, titled "Research, Technological Development and Innovation and Other Provisions." Authority in Charge of Development: The policy was developed under the auspices of the Hellenic Ministry of Education, Research, and Religious Affairs. Since 2019, due to restructuring of the public administration bodies, amendments have been undertaken by the Ministry of Investments. GSRI has been at the forefront of these activities from the beginning (before 2019 named General Secretariat for Research and Technology – GSRT). Entities Involved in Development: The formulation of Law 4310/2014 involved multiple stakeholders, including: •	General Secretariat for Research and Innovation: Provided strategic direction and oversight. •	Greek Atomic Energy Commission (EEAE): Contributed expertise, particularly for sections related to nuclear energy and radiation protection. •	Research and Academic Institutions: Offered insights and feedback during the drafting process. •	National Council for Research and Technology (NCRT-ESET) Process of Development: The development of Law 4310/2014 followed a structured process, starting with consultations involving scientific communities, research institutions, and industry stakeholders. This was followed by drafting a policy framework aligned with national research and innovation goals. The draft then underwent review and revision, incorporating feedback from public consultations and expert assessments. Finally, the refined policy was submitted for legislative approval and enacted by the Hellenic Parliament. (A draft analysis in Greek can be found at: https://zenodo.org/records/4502176) Year of Adoption: The law was adopted on December 8, 2014. Year of Last Update: The most recent amendment occurred in 2022, with the enactment of Law 4982/2022, which introduced updates to specific provisions.   Key Areas/Sections of the Policy: •	Research and Innovation Framework: Establishes the national strategy for research, technological development, and innovation. •	Institutional Structure: Defines the roles and responsibilities of research organisations and oversight bodies. •	Funding Mechanisms: Outlines financial support structures for research and innovation activities. •	Pre-Commercial Procurement (PCP): Introduces the concept of PCP to promote innovation through public sector demand.  •	Nuclear Energy and Radiation Protection: Details regulations concerning the management of nuclear energy and assigns regulatory authority to the EEAE.  Webpage Links: •	Full Text of Law 4310/2014: http://www.gsrt.gr/Legislation/Files/LawFiles136/%CE%9D 4310-2014 %CE%B5%CE%BD%CE%BF%CF%80%CE%BF%CE%B9%CE%B7%CE%BC%CE%AD%CE%BD%CE%BF%CF%82.pdf (in Greek) •	Explanatory Memorandum: https://www.minedu.gov.gr/publications/docs2015/Explanatory_Memorandum_to_Law_4310_2014.pdf (in English)

### B2.2 — Please, if possible, briefly explain the reason and the difficulties or obstacles in this area and indicate if there are plans to do so

Greece faces a unique challenge in Open Science governance due to the distribution of responsibilities across three ministries. As highlighted during the 3rd Open Science Symposium, this structure requires clear guidelines and enhanced coordination, particularly in the ongoing update of the Digital Transformation Bible. Strengthening inter-ministerial collaboration is crucial to avoiding past gaps, such as the limited involvement of the Ministry of Development and the Ministry of Education, Religious Affairs, and Sports in Open Science discussions due to their exclusion from relevant processes.

### B2.3 — Please, if possible, briefly explain the reason and the difficulties or obstacles in this area and indicate if there are plans to do so

The GSRI is actively working to strengthen coordination and secure funding for the successful development and implementation of Open Science policies in Greece. Particularly, efforts are underway to enable the Hellenic Open Science Initiative (HOSI) to implement concrete actions from the national plan. However, these efforts are still at a preliminary stage.

### B2.4 — Please provide details and links as relevant, including the name of ministries and national authorities/entities, as well as affiliated organizations that are involved in the development and implementation of the plan, strategy or the roadmap

The GSRI is actively working to strengthen coordination and secure funding that will enable the adoption of the National Open Science Plan as a national strategy.

### B2.5 — Please provide more information, including the number of policies, name of the institute(s) that has/have developed the policy(s) and elements of open science included in the policy (please consider policies and strategies addressing all elements of open science, including citizen
and participatory  science, or co-production of knowledge with multiple actors).

Five universities in Greece have already adopted institutional Open Access (OA) policies (https://roarmap.eprints.org/view/country/300.html), while all universities maintain self-archiving policies linked to their institutional repositories, ensuring the preservation of Open Access publications. The HOSI plays a pivotal role in harmonizing the adoption of Open Science policies across institutions. As a result, more universities are expressing interest in joining HOSI, further accelerating institutional adoption. This momentum is also reinforced by the 71st Rectors' Conference, where Open Access and Open Science were officially recognized, driving a coordinated and systematic approach to policy implementation in Greek universities.

### B2.6 — Please provide details and links as relevant

While there is no dedicated national financial strategy, the Hellenic Academic Libraries Link (HEAL-Link) serves as the national body responsible for negotiating agreements with scientific publishers, ensuring access to scientific information for the Greek research community. HEAL-Link also facilitates Open Access agreements that waive publication fees. These agreements are primarily funded through national resources from the Ministry, but individual institutions may also allocate local budgets for specific electronic resource acquisitions that meet their needs. More details are available at: HEAL-Link Open Access Agreements. In addition, some funding for electronic infrastructures has been allocated to GRNET, the National Infrastructures for Research and Technology provider in Greece, for enhancing capacity to support research.

### B2.7 — Please provide details and links as relevant

There are recent efforts to include Open Science requirements in calls for projects of the Hellenic Foundation for Research & Innovation (HFRI) and EREVNO KAINOTOMO of GSRI, with a special focus on Open Access to publications.

### B2.8 — Please, if possible, briefly explain the reason and the difficulties or obstacles in this area and indicate if there are plans to do so

There is strong engagement of the policy community to adopt Open Science in the public sector. Once Greece has this foundation at national level, more actions towards PPPs are expected to be planned.

### B2.9 — Please provide details and links as relevant

Greece is actively contributing to the development of the EOSC Observatory, which tracks national progress on Open Science policy implementation, funding, infrastructures, skills, and engagement with the European Open Science Cloud (EOSC). While this is not a national monitoring mechanism per se, the GSRI is working towards a structured approach to monitoring Open Science activities in Greece by exploring best practices, such as the Irish Open Science Monitor developed by OpenAIRE, which provides a structured framework for tracking Open Science adoption. Furthermore, HEAL-Link has been monitoring Open Access to publications through surveys and reports (available here: https://scholarly.heal-link.gr/monitoring/) and is currently developing an OpenAIRE Monitor for its academic community.

### B3.4 — For each infrastructure, please provide links as relevant and more information with regard to their compliance with the 2021 Recommendation on Open Science in terms of inclusivity, accessibility, interoperability, community governance, FAIR (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable) and CARE (Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility and Ethics) principles. Please indicate if the infrastructure is non-commercial

Greece has several national and institutional Open Science infrastructures that support preservation, management, and open access to scientific information. ATHENA RC and GRNET have developed the HELIX (Hellenic Data Service) infrastructure to address long-term data preservation needs. HELIX, recognized as one of the core national research infrastructures in Greece’s National Roadmap for Research Infrastructures, is designed according to Open Science and Open Source best practices, ensuring compliance with OpenAIRE guidelines for interoperability and supporting FAIR data principles. HARDMIN, a twin infrastructure to HELIX developed by ATHENA RC, is managed by HEAL-Link and serves Greek universities by providing interconnected services for research data storage and reuse. In addition, HEAL-Link maintains a literature repositories network, enhancing access to scientific information. ARGOS, OpenAIRE’s machine-actionable Data Management Planning (DMP) service, has been deployed in HEAL-Link’s infrastructure, integrating with HARDMIN and academic services to automate and validate the DMP process while promoting early-stage data sharing. Beyond institutional repositories, Greek Nodes of Research Infrastructures, such as SODANET and ELIXIR-GR, contribute by offering services catalogues and developing best practices for Research Data Management (RDM) and FAIR data implementation in their designated domains.  Overall, while significant progress has been made, continued funding and policy support are essential to enhance and sustain these Open Science infrastructures.

## B4 – Education, Capacity Building & Science Communication

### B4.1 — Please indicate if those capacity building initiatives are formal or informal and provide more information and links as relevant

There have been multiple bottom-up initiatives aimed at upskilling researchers, academic librarians, and research support staff with Open Science expertise. Both HEAL-Link and Athena Research Center serving as the OpenAIRE NOAD play a crucial role in training and capacity building for Open Science in Greece. Through a series of webinars and training events, they provide practical guidance on Research Data Management (RDM), data management planning, FAIR principles, repository use, interoperability, and compliance with funding requirements, including Horizon Europe mandates. HEAL-Link’s Scholarly Communication Unit webinars focus on supporting researchers, librarians, and institutional policymakers in adopting Open Science practices while OpenAIRE complements these efforts by engaging research institutions, funders, and service providers across Greece and Europe, ensuring the seamless integration of Open Science principles into the research ecosystem. In 2022, the RDM Training Programme for Academic Librarians was offered by OpenAIRE NOAD in Greece, focused on FAIR data principles, data curation, and best practices for supporting researchers in managing research data. This initiative strengthened the role of academic libraries in Open Science by providing tailored guidance and practical training. More lately, the HOSI Training Working Group coordinated systematic Open Science training activities across Greek institutions-HOSI members. The group has produced training resources and will be organizing workshops, webinars, and knowledge-sharing sessions to support the adoption of Open Science in 2025.  Lastly, HOSI members participating in European Open Science projects might also perform training activities of sorts in 2025.

## B5 – Research Assessment and Incentives

### B5.1 — Please provide more information and links as relevant

While significant progress has been made by individual organisations in the context of their signatory and participation to CoARA, challenges remain in embedding these principles into formal evaluation frameworks. More specifically: •	ATHENA Research Center (ARC) is the only Greek organisation to join CoARA and is currently working towards updating its policies to adopt responsible research assessment (RRA) practices. •	The National and Kapodistrian University of Athens (NKUA) closely monitors CoARA developments as a signatory, preparing for potential alignment with Open Science-friendly evaluation practices. •	A national CoARA Chapter is currently being developed, led by ARC and supported by HOSI, to coordinate efforts and foster institutional engagement in research assessment reform. •	The Hellenic Authority for Higher Education (HAHE) is aware of Open Science principles, but these are not yet integrated into its monitoring and evaluation processes.

## B7 – International Cooperation

### B7.1 — How is your country promoting and facilitating international scientific collaborations on open science as outlined in the 2021 Recommendation on Open Science?

Greece facilitates international scientific collaborations on Open Science, as outlined in the 2021 Recommendation on Open Science (vii) 22.a, primarily through its involvement in the Research Data Alliance (RDA) and the international outreach of ESFRI (European Strategy Forum on Research Infrastructures). HOSI members actively contribute to RDA working groups and interest groups (notably CERTH and Athena Research Center have been leading at least 5 groups), helping to shape global best practices in FAIR data and services, reproducibility, software and research data management (RDM). These collaborations and outputs are then communicated to HOSI General Assembly ensuring alignment with global Open Science strategies. At the same time, Greece’s participation in ESFRI RIs extends its Open Science outreach across multiple disciplines. Through ELIXIR-GR, Greece supports open bioinformatics data sharing, while DARIAH-GR & CLARIN:EL enhance digital research in humanities and linguistics. CESSDA-GR facilitates cross-border social science data sharing, and EPOS-GR advances the open accessibility and interoperability of geological and seismological data. These collaborations reinforce Greece’s commitment to Open Science by integrating its research infrastructures into the broader European and international ecosystem.
