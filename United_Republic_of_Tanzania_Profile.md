## B1 – Promotion of the UNESCO 2021 Recommendation on Open Science

### B1.1 — If yes, please indicate which ministries and or national authorities/entities have been involved in the promotion of the 2021 Recommendation

Yes, the Ministry has disseminated and promoted the 2021 recommendations to relevant ministries and institutions through various workshops and seminars. For instance, in March 2023, the Ministry of Education, Science and Technology (MoEST), through the Tanzania Commission for Science and Technology (COSTECH), organized a National Open Science Dialogue to explore the challenges and opportunities associated with establishing an open science ecosystem in Tanzania. This dialogue underscored the need for a national open science policy to guide research dissemination, data sharing, and incentivize open science practices. Furthermore, in December 2024, MoEST conducted an open science awareness-raising seminar for staff members of universities and Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET) institutions.

### B1.3 — If yes, please provide details on the activities organized or foreseen and links as relevant

Yes, there have been awareness-raising activities on open science, although the landscape of such initiatives varies across institutions. The Ministry of Education, Science and Technology (MoEST) has organized awareness-raising workshops for Higher Learning Institutions (HLIs). Additionally, institutions such as the Nelson Mandela African Institution of Science and Technology (NM-AIST), the Mining Commission, the Tanzania Fisheries Research Institute (TAFIRI), the Tanzania Gemological Centre (TGC), and the Tanzania Meteorological Authority (TMA) have actively conducted internal workshops and training sessions to promote open science practices.

### B1.4 — If yes, please indicate the actions that are taken to incorporate the specific values and principles. For each case, please provide details and links as relevant

Several institutions have undertaken concrete measures to integrate open science principles into publicly funded research. For example, the Tanzania Commission for Science and Technology (COSTECH) has established open-access institutional repositories and requires researchers to publish their findings in open-access journals, thereby promoting transparency and accessibility. Similarly, the Tanzania Gemological Centre (TGC) has incorporated open science practices into its research funding mechanisms, supported by government funding for infrastructure development. In addition, the Nelson Mandela African Institution of Science and Technology (NM-AIST) encourages researchers to adhere to open science principles when applying for funding. A notable example is the recently submitted proposal titled “FAIR-DATASHARE: Enabling Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable (FAIR) Research Data Sharing among Higher Education Institutions (HEIs) Researchers in Tanzania Using Research Data Repositories (RDRs),” submitted to the VLIR-UOS Short Initiative Call 2025. Furthermore, institutions such as Kampala International University in Tanzania (KIUT) have adopted open science practices as a prerequisite for funding, particularly for health-related research funded by the European Union.

## B2 – Open Science Policies and Governance

### B2.5 — Please provide more information, including the number of policies, name of the institute(s) that has/have developed the policy(s) and elements of open science included in the policy (please consider policies and strategies addressing all elements of open science, including citizen
and participatory  science, or co-production of knowledge with multiple actors).

Some institutions, like Tanzania Commission for Science and Technology (COSTECH) and Kampala International University in Tanzania (KIUT), have established policies and strategies aligned with the 2021 UNESCO Recommendation on Open Science. COSTECH, for instance, has developed a National Data Sharing Framework for Science, Technology and Innovation and promotes participatory science through public involvement in research projects. Similarly, KIUT’s research policy emphasizes community involvement and participatory research. However, many institutions operate under broader national policies that but lack standalone institutional policies. For example, TGC aligns with Tanzania’s National Research and Development Policy but does not have a specific open science policy.

### B2.7 — Please provide details and links as relevant

Some, institutions like COSTECH and KIUT have successfully integrated open science practices into their research funding mechanisms, emphasizing open-access publishing and data sharing. COSTECH’s funding guidelines require researchers to disseminate their findings through open-access platforms, promoting transparency and collaboration. Similarly, KIUT’s research funding, particularly from the European Union, mandates open science practices as a precondition for funding.

### B2.8 — Please provide details and links as relevant

Efforts to foster public-private partnerships (PPPs) on open science are underway. Institutions like TGC and COSTECH, which have signed MoUs and engaged in collaborative projects with private sector organizations. TGC, for example, has partnered with the Gemmological Association of Great Britain (Gem-A) to enhance gemological education and research. Similarly, COSTECH’s strategic plan emphasizes increasing public-private partnership engagement in science, technology, and innovation.

## B7 – International Cooperation

### B7.1 — How is your country promoting and facilitating international scientific collaborations on open science as outlined in the 2021 Recommendation on Open Science?

Tanzania is actively promoting and facilitating international scientific collaborations on open science, aligning with the 2021 UNESCO Recommendation on Open Science. The Tanzania Commission for Science and Technology (COSTECH) is leading efforts to develop a national open science policy that harmonizes existing policies and provides a cohesive framework for open science practices across the country. Collaborations with organizations like the Consortium of Tanzania Universities and Research Libraries (COTUL) and Electronic Information for Libraries (EIFL) have been instrumental in these developments. Additionally, COSTECH has organized training workshops to enhance researchers' skills in securing research funding and adopting open science practices, including participants from both Mainland Tanzania and Zanzibar. Efforts are also underway to improve open science infrastructure, such as establishing institutional repositories and adopting open journal systems, to make research outputs more accessible and promote data sharing among researchers. Furthermore, Tanzania has participated in regional dialogues, such as the East African Community's initiatives on open science, to address challenges and define strategies for implementing open science practices. Through these concerted efforts, Tanzania is making significant strides in aligning with the UNESCO Recommendation on Open Science, fostering an environment conducive to international scientific collaboration and the advancement of open science principles.

### B7.3 — Please provide more information and links as relevant

Tanzania is actively engaged in discussions and initiatives aimed at establishing regional and international funding mechanisms to support open science, in alignment with the 2021 UNESCO Recommendation on Open Science. As highlighted above, in February 2023, a High-Level Multi-Sectoral National Open Science Dialogue was convened in Tanzania, bringing together stakeholders from academia, research institutions, and policy-making bodies. This dialogue, co-hosted by the Tanzania Commission for Science and Technology (COSTECH), the East African Science and Technology Commission (EASTECO), the Public Library of Science (PLOS), and the Training Centre in Communication Africa, focused on strategies to promote open science and explored avenues for securing funding from both governmental and international partners. Additionally, Tanzania's participation in regional collaborations, such as the African Union (AU), East African Community (EAC), and Southern African Development Community (SADC), underscores its commitment to fostering international cooperation and mobilizing resources to advance open science initiatives.

## B8 – Other

### B8.1 — Please provide more information and links as relevant

Financial constraints often limit funding for open science, as many funders continue to prioritize traditional research models. Inadequate digital infrastructure, particularly unreliable internet access, further hinders the effective implementation of open science practices. Additionally, existing national and regional policies may not fully support open science, creating regulatory obstacles. Some researchers are also hesitant to adopt open approaches due to concerns over intellectual property rights and data privacy. Compounding these challenges is a general lack of awareness and training on the benefits of open science, highlighting the need for targeted capacity building and support for researchers
